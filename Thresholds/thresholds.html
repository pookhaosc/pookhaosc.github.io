Thresholds

Growing up, one of my favourite pastimes I did at school was exploring the nooks and crannies and other places I wasn’t meant to. From the underground utility vault to the corridor at the back of one of the school buildings which led to the caretaker’s room, I and a few friends spent our lunch breaks avoiding getting caught by teachers on lunch duty. The combination of the excitement of exploration and not knowing what to expect, the fear of getting caught, and the comfort in solidarity – that I wasn’t alone – was a special feeling.

My school used to host a Scholastic Book Fair every year, and I was always fond of the I Spy and Can You See What I See? book series. Each page of a book has a photograph with hidden objects and accompanying riddles that tell you which objects to find. I’d always liked the photographs. There was something that felt slightly off – a subtle uncanny quality that was hard to quantify. A bowling pin on an antique fence gate, a bed with way too many stuffed animals to be comforting, or a clock with 13 hours. They were like windows to an altered reality.

A few years later, I graduated secondary school, said goodbye to my family in Bangkok, and boarded a plane to the Netherlands to start a new chapter of my life at the University of Twente. It was a hectic week; I remember packing my bags the day of my flight after getting the confirmation of my admission way too late. Everything felt so abrupt. On one hand, I had been looking forward to a change for a while and was excited to move. On the other hand, I guess I didn’t really have time to process everything that was happening. There was the thrill of moving forward and not knowing what's ahead, but also – in the back of my mind – the wistful nostalgia for things I was about to leave behind.

What all these snippets have in common what I’d best describe as the thrill of existing in the present while moving forwards towards – or through – changes. A touch of childlike wonder and curiosity of not knowing what to expect; a slightly disorienting sense of liminality; a personal journey across thresholds of time and space. This photography project aims to capture and portray these emotions.

Onwards

From a cross-country road trip to a peaceful morning stall, travelling is quite literally about transitioning, both physically and mentally. I have some hazy memory of sitting in the back seat of my parents’ car, watching the world rushes by. Sometimes I’d fall into a light sleep and wake up feeling groggy and disoriented. I’d look out the window and it was already dark and, for a few seconds, I had no idea where we were.

Is there anybody home?

From an eerily-quiet ghost town to a house with locked doors and drawn curtains, places made for people look strangely unsettling when deserted. What’s behind that closed door? Where is everybody? The sense of uncertainty and ambiguity can be both exciting and disorienting.
